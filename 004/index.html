brahms, violin concerto in d major

  • 2:55 the orchestral build up to the soloist entrance (3:16). just imagine the violinist has been on stage for 3 and a half minutes now, playing nothing. then jumps. double stops. absolute virtuosity. knocking the socks off the bourgeoisie at the 1879 premiere.
  • 24:10 the oboe solo at the start of the second movement is the single most beautiful melody in this piece. then the way the violin picks it up (26:04) and sweetens, embellishes and develops it.

mozart, the marriage of figaro

  • act 3 sull'aria, the countess and susanna's duet. when they finish trading lines and their voices merge. iconically used in the most gorgeous scene in shawshank redemption.
  • act 4 contessa perdono. at the end of the opera, the count's plea and her forgiveness. musically sublime and utterly convincing (maybe even despite the plot). as amadeus put it, "the music of true forgiveness, conferring on all who sat there perfect absolution".

beethoven, piano concerto no. 4 in g major

  • 0:12 the solo piano opening line, which is then echoed and lifted up by the entrance of the orchestra. crazy how the same rhythm pattern as the main theme of beethoven 5 (which sounds aggressive and triumphant) instead here sounds so tender, feels like beethoven telling you a secret. mitskuo kills this.
  • 21:03 the soft piano and aggressive orchestra interacting (or more like ignoring each other) at the start of the second movement. then the solo part (23:40) after the orchestra quiets down.

rachmaninoff, piano concerto no. 2 in c minor

  • 0:02 the opening piano chords in the karajan/richter recording. the tempo he choses and the execution are absoltutely masterful, and the way the piano punctuates the orchestral melody.
  • 14:23 the main theme of the second movement is stunning, especially its final restatement by the orchestra (20:19). the strings' final chord progression (21:47) are shiver inducing.

mozart, requiem mass

  • 19:11 confutatis maledictis: the strings go brr. also the contrast of the voca me (19:30).
  • 24:15 lacrimosa: the amen fugue in the robert levin completion is stellar.

brahms, horn trio in e flat major, op. 40

  • 5:29 in the third movement, the high peak of the horn melody (6:13) after the build up feels like reaching the top of a mountain after a strenuous hike.

radiohead, how to disappear completely [kid a]

  • 0:20 ok i LOVE this song. starting with the eerie strings and ondes martenot setting the mood. the walking bassline (starting 0:20) that anchors the song. the sparse lyrics and backstory.
  • 4:26 the true highlight is the final minute and a half. the way thom yorke's final "i'm not here" sets off his voice blending into and becoming one of the many strings. the full breakdown of tonality (5:04) where the lines of weaving strings and ondes martenot and voice float and disintegrate further and further until, finally, somehow, everything coalesces back (5:23) into a beautiful pure e major, like the sun breaking out behind clouds. everything is good.

radiohead, exit music (for a film) [ok computer]

  • 2:35 the chill in his voice at "such a chill" followed by the masterful drop at 2:50 into "you can laugh" that's been (understandably) used in so many film scores.
  • 4:08 the fade and the absolute tenderness in delivery of "we hope that you choke".

queen, i want to break free [the works]

  • 0:19 iconic opening. the rhythm drop at the start. really made iconic from that one scene in bbc sherlock.

emerson lake & palmer, knife-edge [emerson lake & palmer]

  • 3:20 the quote of bach's french suite is so unexpected and fun.

peggy lee, is that all there is?

  • 0:35 this song is an instant teleporation button back to sleep no more. from the very first second. but especially when the instrumentation enters and she starts singing.

alfa mist, galaxy [blue note re:imagined]

  • 0:00 the opening with eerie synth-keys-horns-weird textures sets the mood so well, and then the rhythm drop (1:15) is so masterful.
  • 8:00 the way near the end of the piece, the horn theme from the beginning subtly slides back in, under the wavery high notes.

joni mitchell, amelia [hejira] / [shadows and light]

  • 0:55 the way the guitar enters on "the drone of flying engines" and brings this airy floating quality, mirroring the lyrics. the entire song is gorgeous, with stunning chord progressions and lyrical writing.
  • 6:20 pat metheny's guitar solo in the live album recording sounds so ethereal and tells a full story.

nina simone, feeling good [i put a spell on you]

  • 0:35 the downward slide of her voice into the first "feeling good", cueing the simultaneous drop of the horns. a classic for a reason.

vulfpeck, live at MSG

  • 0:47 backpocket: the way the crowd pulls out a three part harmony immediately, with zero sweat. the energy of that entire performance. peak internet content. reliably smile inducing.
  • 0:07 deantown: to quote a youtube comment, "14,000 strong crowd singing a bassline, the true American dream".

stromae, ave cesaria [racine carrée]

  • 0:33 the first and second verse are just so lyrically well crafted and rhythmically interesting. "les effluves de rum dans ta voix me font tourner la tête / tu me fais danser du bout des doigts, comme tes cigarettes".

sinead o'connor, just like u said it would b [the lion and the cobra]

  • 0:26 the hints at raspiness in her voice, and the way the three note guitar strum motif intersperse "to sink and to swim"
  • 3:18 her voice tone shift from soft head voice into this raspy tone and the scream at 3:43. absolutely astonishing

jacob collier, time alone with you [djesse, vol 3]

  • 3:18 "do you really wanna call / yeah i really wanna call your name" the question being answered before it's even done being formulated is SO cute. and then the little bass lick.

lorde, helen of troy [solar power]

rosalía, sakura [motomami]
